Building Your Union

Our union's logo.

The first and hardest step of organizing your co-workers is talking to them. Your first goal, as an orgainizer, should be identifying who might share your passion for making your workplace better. Have lots of conversations about work (you probably already do) and find out what people care about---how do your friends want to see your environment change? Also keep in mind who will make a good organizer. Everyone will get involved eventually, and it's good to keep a mental list of what is important to everyone, but at early stages you need to focus on growing your reach. Nobody can build a union alone!

Something you definitely want to avoid is becoming a 'union guy' at this point. Just take every opportunity to plant the seeds in peoples' minds. What would you change about work if you could? Do you think that admin will ever make the changes you want to see? Did you hear what happened to so-and-so? I wish we could have helped them. After a few conversations, you'll have a feel of whether you should say the word 'union' or not with a particular person. That's when you invite them to the organizing meetings.

Our first meetings were in cheap bars where everyone could get a beer. This was fine at first, but eventually you want them to stop feeling like a hang-out and start feeling like work. The first thing you need is a list of graduate students at your university. This can be harder to get than you might expect! At OHSU, there is no good, continuously updated, publicly available directory of employees. We mostly worked with a list I scraped from the employee directory. It was out of date, and I had to write a (pretty horrendous) script to deal with it, but it's better than nothing.

Once you have a list, start assigning people. Eventually, you'll want 10% of your bargaining unit to be involved in the organizational aspect, since any more than that and people start to be spread out too thin. Have a meeting where everyone takes a group of people they're responsible for and will be for the forseeable future. This point is key---you need to keep talking to people! Someone you might think is a yes one week will have a bad conversation with someone they know whose parent was in a union in 1980 and they're a no all of a sudden. Keep checking in!

Around this point, you'll want to start contacting unions you think you'd like to work with. I'd recommend finding one that is, at least in theory, democratically set up. You don't want someone who has never seen a test tube setting priorities for your labwork job. You'll definitely want someone who's willing to assign you an organizer, part-time at first, but once you start to get past the initial hurdle (for us I think it was around 15 or 20 people?) you'd want someone full-time dedicated to keeping things organized and moving.

Once you've got your organizer (or even your committee), stop listening to me! They know what to do better than me---this is just how we ended up going about things. However, you can always shoot me an email, twitter DM, or even a letter about this type of stuff. I strongly believe that making our universities better, safer places for all to do their research makes better science!
